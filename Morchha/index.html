A morchha is a defensive position held when retreat would have been easier — a stand taken not because victory is certain but because surrender is unacceptable. It is the barricade built from whatever is at hand: conviction, stubbornness, desperation, love. Morchhā asks you to name the positions you held — the stands you took when the easier path was to walk away.

Morchhā

The morchha is a term from battle — a fortified position, a line drawn in sand or stone. In Indian protest movements, a morchha is where people sit and refuse to move. They build no walls; their bodies are the barricade. The power of the morchha is not in weapons but in the willingness to endure discomfort for a cause.

You have built morchhas in your own life. The argument you refused to lose because the principle mattered more than peace. The relationship you fought for when everyone told you to let go. The boundary you enforced even when it made you unpopular. These stands cost you something. They always do.

Name the barricades. Know what you defended and what it cost you to hold the line.
