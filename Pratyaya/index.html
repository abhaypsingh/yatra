trust

Trust is invisible. You cannot point to it or hold it. But the moment it breaks, you feel it like a bone.

And like a bone, it can heal. Sometimes stronger than before.

Six stories about trust — broken, rebuilt, misplaced, earned, given too fast, and withheld too long.

Trust is a choice.

It is not a feeling that arrives on its own. It is a decision you make, again and again, to believe that the risk of being hurt is worth the reward of being close to someone.

Every person you love will disappoint you at some point. Not because they are bad, but because they are human. The question is never whether trust will be tested. It always will. The question is: what do you do after?

You rebuild. Plank by plank. Not because it is easy, but because the alternative — a life without trust — is a life without depth, without closeness, without the thing that makes being human worth it.

Pratyaya प्रत्यय

Pratyaya means trust, confidence, firm conviction. In yoga philosophy, it is the content of consciousness — the beliefs that shape how you see the world.

Research on trust shows it is built in small moments, not grand gestures. A kept promise. A phone call returned. Showing up when you said you would. Trust is the accumulation of a thousand tiny choices to be reliable.

When trust breaks, it can be rebuilt. But the repair requires honesty about what happened, accountability for the damage, and time. Always time.
