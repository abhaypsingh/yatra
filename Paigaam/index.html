Before the message arrives, you are one person. After, you are another. The telegram that brought news of a death. The handwritten note slipped under a door that said I forgive you. The phone call where three words — it is benign — turned dread into euphoria. Messages are the hinges of life, the precise instants where the story pivots.

Some messages you sent. The apology you laboured over for hours, choosing each word as though it were a brick in a bridge. The confession you typed and deleted six times before finally pressing send. The goodbye you could only manage in writing because your voice would have broken.

Collect the messages that mattered. Received or sent, they are the words that proved language can change the shape of a life.
