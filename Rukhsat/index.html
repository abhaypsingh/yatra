Rukhsat

the farewell

Rukhsat is the formal farewell — the departure that is not sudden but ceremonial, witnessed by others, sanctioned by ritual. It is the bride leaving her father's house, the coffin lowered while prayers are recited, the last day at a home you will never return to. There is a terrible beauty in a goodbye that everyone agrees is final. Rukhsat asks you to name the farewells that were performed, not stolen — the departures where the world stopped to watch you leave or to watch someone leave you.

Rukhsat

Most goodbyes happen without warning — a phone call, a silence that stretches into permanence, a door that simply does not open again. But a rukhsat is different. It is the farewell you see coming, the one that gathers people in a room and asks them to face the leaving together. There are tears, but they are expected. There are embraces, but they have an end.

The weight of rukhsat is that it asks you to be present for the exact moment something ends. You cannot look away. You cannot pretend it is temporary. The ritual insists that you feel the full measure of the departure — and in doing so, it gives the grief a shape, a beginning and an end, a frame within which sorrow is permitted to exist.
