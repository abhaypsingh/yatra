A ghaat is the stone steps descending to a river — where the city meets the water. At the ghaat, the living bathe, the dead are cremated, children play, and priests chant. It is where life meets what is larger than life. Ghāṭ asks you to name the descents to the water where you washed away what needed releasing.

Ghāṭ

The ghaat is the threshold between the built world and the elemental. Above the steps: the city, with its streets and shops and noise. Below: the river, which was here before the city and will be here after. To descend the ghaat is to leave the human world and touch something older, something that does not care about your name or your troubles but will take them from you if you let it.

Water transforms. The living are cleansed, the dead are released, the grieving are consoled. The river does not judge what you bring to it. It simply receives, carries, and moves on. And you walk back up the steps lighter than when you came down.

Name the descents. The water remembers what you released, even if you have forgotten.
