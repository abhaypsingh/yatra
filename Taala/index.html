Tālā

the lock

A taala keeps things safe or keeps people out — the same mechanism serves protection and exclusion. The lock does not judge; it simply holds. What matters is who has the key and what they choose to do with it. Tālā asks you to name the locks of your life — the things that were sealed away, whether for safekeeping or for silence.

Tālā

A lock on a trunk holds a grandmother’s jewellery safe. A lock on a diary holds a teenager’s secrets. A lock on a door holds a family apart. The mechanism is identical in each case — a bolt, a key, a click. But the meaning changes entirely depending on which side of the lock you stand.

You have locked things away — memories you didn’t want to revisit, truths you weren’t ready to speak, parts of yourself you hid for safekeeping. And you have stood outside locked doors, wanting in, lacking the key. Both are the taala.

Name the locks. Decide which ones still need to hold, and which ones you are ready to open.
