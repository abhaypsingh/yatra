खोमचा

Khomchā

the street vendor’s cart

A khomchā is the small wheeled cart or shoulder-slung tray of the street vendor — the man who sells roasted peanuts at the park gate, chaat at the bus stand, or bangles at the temple steps. He comes to you. He does not wait for you to enter his shop; he has no shop. His entire livelihood is balanced on two wheels or on his shoulders. Khomchā asks you to name the things that came to you — the nourishments, lessons, and joys that arrived at your doorstep without you having to seek them out.

▮

Khomchā

The khomchā vendor is the economy of proximity. He studies the rhythms of the street: when schoolchildren emerge hungry, when office workers need tea, when evening walkers want roasted corn. He does not advertise. He simply appears at the right place, at the right time, with exactly what is wanted. The transaction takes thirty seconds. Both parties walk away satisfied.

Your khomchās were the arrivals. The friend who called on exactly the right day. The book that fell into your hands at exactly the right moment. The word of encouragement that rolled up to your doorstep just when you were about to quit. You did not search for these things. They found you.

Name the carts. Some of the most important provisions of your life were delivered, not sought.
