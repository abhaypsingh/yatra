Murabbā

the preserve

Murabba is fruit preserved in sugar syrup — amla, carrot, apple — cooked slowly until the fruit absorbs the sweetness completely. It takes days to make, and it lasts for months. The fruit that would have rotted in a week now endures through seasons. Murabbā asks you to name the things you have preserved against time — the memories, traditions, or qualities you sweetened and sealed so they would not decay.

Murabbā

Making murabba is an act of faith in the future. You take something perishable and spend days transforming it into something that will last. You add sweetness. You apply heat. You wait. And when the jar is finally sealed, you have defeated time — at least for one fruit, for one season.

You make murabbas too. The stories you retell to keep them alive. The values you sweeten with context so your children will accept them. The memories you consciously preserve because you know time will try to take them.

Name the preserves. They are how you fight impermanence with sweetness.
