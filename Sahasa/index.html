Sāhasa

courage

Courage is not the absence of fear. It is the decision that something else matters more.

Name what frightens you. Look at it directly. Walk through it slowly: what is the worst that could happen? What would you do? What would surviving it teach you?

A fear examined is a fear halved.

step one

Name it

What are you afraid of right now? It can be big or small. Say it plainly.

step two

The worst that could happen

If this fear came true — the absolute worst version — what would that look like? Write it down. Let it be real for a moment.

step three

What would you do?

Even in that worst case — you would still be here. What would you actually do? Who would you turn to? What is your first step?

step four

What surviving it would teach you

If you went through it and came out the other side — what would you know about yourself that you do not know now?

✦

You looked at it.

You named your fear. You imagined the worst. You made a plan. You found what it would teach you.

That is courage. Not the absence of fear — but the willingness to face it.

Sāhasa साहस

Sāhasa means courage, daring, boldness. In Sanskrit literature, it is one of the essential virtues — not recklessness, but the willingness to face what is difficult.

This practice comes from a simple truth: fear grows in the dark. When you look at it directly — name it, imagine the worst, make a plan, find the lesson — it shrinks. Not to nothing. But to something you can carry.
