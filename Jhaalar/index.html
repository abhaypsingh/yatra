Jhālar

the fringe of light

Jhālar is the string of decorative lights — the fringe of tiny bulbs strung along rooftops and doorways during festivals. Each bulb is small and ordinary alone, but strung together they transform the outline of a house into a thing of wonder. The jhālar does not illuminate; it celebrates. It traces the shape of something and says: this matters, this is worth decorating. Jhālar asks you to name the fringes of light in your life — the small, strung-together joys that outlined something ordinary and made it feel like a festival.

Jhālar

A single bulb is just a point of light. But string fifty of them along a roofline and suddenly the house has a crown. The jhālar’s magic is in the accumulation: each light is trivial, but the series is magnificent. This is how celebration often works — not through one grand gesture but through many small ones arranged with intention.

Your jhālars were the accumulated small joys. The nightly cup of tea that made every evening a small festival. The series of letters from a friend. The chain of tiny traditions — the Sunday walk, the birthday song, the bedtime story — that strung together to outline the shape of a whole childhood, a whole marriage, a whole life.
