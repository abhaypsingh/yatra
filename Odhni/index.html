Odhnī

the shawl

An odhni is a light shawl — not armour, not disguise, but something softer. It drapes across the shoulders or the head, offering modesty or grace without the weight of concealment. It covers just enough to leave something to the imagination, just enough to feel held. Odhnī asks you to name the things you wrapped around yourself — not walls but veils, the soft protections that let you move through the world without being fully exposed.

Odhnī

There is a difference between hiding and choosing what to reveal. An odhni does not build walls — it offers a translucence, a softness between you and the world. It is the habit you cultivated to seem calm, the politeness that shielded your grief, the composure that let you survive rooms full of people who did not know what you were carrying.

An odhni can be beautiful. The way it catches wind, the way it frames a face. But it can also be a weight disguised as lightness — something you wore so long you forgot what you looked like without it. Grace, too, can become a cage when it is never allowed to fall.

Name the shawls. Honour both their beauty and their burden.
