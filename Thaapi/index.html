Thāpī

the hand-stamp

A thaapi is a hand pressed firmly into wet cow-dung to make a flat cake for fuel — or a child’s hand dipped in colour and pressed against a wall for Diwali. It is the most primal mark: this hand was here. This person existed. This moment was real. Thāpī asks you to name the handprints you have left in the wet surfaces of your life — the marks that say you were here and you mattered.

Thāpī

The handprint is humanity’s oldest art. Cave walls in Bhimbetka bear them from thirty thousand years ago. A hand dipped in ochre, pressed against stone. No words, no symbols, just: I was here. My hand is this shape. I existed. The thaapi is the same impulse: to leave evidence of presence in a world that will otherwise forget.

You have pressed your thaapi into many surfaces. The organisations you shaped. The children you raised. The friendships you built. The work that carries your invisible fingerprint. These marks may fade. But they were made with your full hand, pressed firmly.

Name the hand-stamps. They are your evidence against forgetting.
