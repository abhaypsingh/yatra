The spectacle — events so dramatic they drew a crowd, whether joyous celebration or public unraveling. A tamaasha can be a wedding procession that stops traffic or an argument that spills into the street. Tamāshā

Life mostly happens in private. But occasionally, something bursts through the walls and becomes public — a tamaasha. It can be glorious: a wedding baraat dancing through the streets, a cricket match won in the last over, a child’s first performance on stage. Or it can be searing: a family feud erupting at a gathering, a public humiliation, a loss so great it cannot be contained by decorum.

The tamaasha is the moment when life becomes theatre without anyone writing a script. You are simultaneously living it and watching it, feeling it and narrating it. These are the memories that come with a crowd attached — because a tamaasha, by definition, has witnesses.

Name the spectacles. Both the ones that made you proud to be seen and the ones you wished no one had witnessed.
