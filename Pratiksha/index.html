Pratīkṣā

waiting

Some of the heaviest things you carry are not memories or feelings.

They are the things that have not happened yet.

Six kinds of waiting. Each one a different weight. And the slow, difficult discovery that your life does not begin when the waiting ends.

the weight of waiting:

You have been waiting a long time.

For things to change. For people to change. For life to start being the version you were promised. Some of those things may come. Some may not. And that uncertainty is one of the hardest things a person can hold.

But here is what the waiting does not want you to know: you are not paused. Even in the limbo. Even in the in-between. You are growing, learning, becoming. The waiting is not wasted time. It is the time when the deepest roots grow.

Your life is not on the other side of the waiting. It is happening right now. In the middle of it. In spite of it.

Pratīkṣā प्रतीक्षा

Pratīkṣā means waiting, expectation, looking forward to. In Sanskrit, it carries a sense of directed attention — the mind oriented toward something that has not yet arrived.

Psychology distinguishes between “active waiting” and “passive waiting.” Passive waiting puts your life on hold. Active waiting — the kind practiced here — means living fully in the present while holding space for what may come.

Many of life’s most important transformations happen not during the events themselves, but during the waiting that precedes them.
