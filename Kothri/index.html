Koṭhrī

the small room

A koṭhrī is the smallest room in the house — sometimes a storeroom, sometimes a prayer room, sometimes the room where valuables are locked away. It is not meant for living in; it is meant for keeping. Every house has a koṭhrī where the most important things are stored behind a closed door. Koṭhrī asks you to name the small, private rooms of your inner life — the locked spaces where you kept the things too precious or too fragile for the open rooms of daily living.

Koṭhrī

The koṭhrī is the opposite of the courtyard. The courtyard is open, shared, public. The koṭhrī is closed, private, and small enough to hold only what truly matters. In old havelis, the koṭhrī held the family jewels, the important documents, the sacred texts. The key was kept by the eldest woman. Not everyone was allowed in.

Your koṭhrīs were the inner rooms. The memory you protect from casual conversation. The dream too fragile for public scrutiny. The grief too private for communal mourning. The joy too personal to share without diluting it. These rooms are not secrets born of shame; they are treasuries born of discernment. Not everything belongs in the open.
