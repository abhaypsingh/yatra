Ghazab

the wrath

Ghazab is anger that has outgrown its container. It is not irritation or frustration — it is the fury that burned through every layer of restraint and emerged as something almost elemental. Sometimes righteous, sometimes ruinous, always unforgettable. Ghazab asks you to name the wraths you have felt or witnessed — the moments when rage broke its banks and flooded everything in its path.

When did your anger become something you could not contain?

Ghazab

We are taught to fear anger, to suppress it, to apologise for it. But some wraths are necessary. The anger of the oppressed is not a character flaw — it is a survival mechanism. The fury of a mother protecting her child is not irrational — it is the most rational force in existence. Ghazab, in its righteous form, is the fire that burns down what should never have been built.

But ghazab also destroys indiscriminately. The rage that made you say the one thing that could never be taken back. The fury that turned you into a version of yourself you did not recognise — shaking, shouting, breaking something that could not be repaired. Afterward, the silence was worse than the noise.

Name your wraths. Examine whether they served justice or fed destruction. Both are yours to claim, and both shaped who you became in their aftermath.
