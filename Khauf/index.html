Khauf

Fear is the oldest part of you. It lived in your body before language, before memory, before the self that calls itself “I.” It made your ancestors climb trees and avoid shadows and survive long enough to create you. But fear did not stop evolving. It learned to attach itself to rejection, to failure, to the imagined judgement of others — threats that carry no teeth but feel mortal.

Some of your fears kept you alive. The instinct that told you not to trust a certain person. The unease that made you check the lock twice. But others kept you small — the terror of being seen, the paralysis before a difficult conversation, the dread that made you choose the safe and familiar over the uncertain and necessary.

Map your fears here. Name them so they become objects you can examine, not shadows that govern you in silence.
