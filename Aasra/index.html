When everything else falls away, there is still one place left — a person whose door is always open, a practice that steadies the shaking hands, a memory that reminds you who you are. Aasra is the refuge you found when the world offered no shelter. Āsrā asks you to name the places, people, and practices that became your last safe ground when you had nowhere else to stand.

Āsrā

Aasra means both shelter and the act of depending on someone. It is the word a mother uses when she says you can always come home. It is the word a friend embodies when they say nothing and simply sit beside you. In India, aasra is not charity — it is duty, it is love, it is the unspoken contract between people who belong to each other. You do not need to ask. The door is already open.

You have known refuges. The aunt’s house you were sent to when things were difficult at home. The book that became a room you could enter when the real room was unbearable. The ritual — a walk, a prayer, a song — that brought you back to yourself when you had drifted too far. These refuges did not solve the problem. They kept you alive until you could face it.

Name the refuges. They are the proof that even in your worst moments, something held you.
