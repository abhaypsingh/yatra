An angithi is a small, open fire in a metal bowl. In winter, families huddle around it, warming their hands, roasting peanuts, talking. It is fire made domestic, made intimate. Angithi asks you to name the small fires that drew people together — the warmth that was shared, not hoarded.

Angiṭhī

The angithi is not grand. It is not a bonfire or a fireplace. It is a small clay or metal bowl with coals. But it is enough. Enough to warm four pairs of hands. Enough to roast a few peanuts. Enough to keep the cold at bay while stories are exchanged.

The best gatherings in life are angithis. Small. Intimate. Warm enough for the people present. Not a party but a huddle. Not entertainment but presence. The crises that brought your family closer. The late-night conversations that nobody planned.

Name the braziers. The small fires that gathered people who mattered.
