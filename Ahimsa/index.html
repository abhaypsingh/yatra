Six moments where the easy path was cruelty and you looked at it clearly — the harm, the cost, and the alternative. That is not passivity. That is the most active form of strength there is.

Gandhi called ahimsa the greatest force at the disposal of humanity. Greater than the most powerful weapon of destruction devised by human ingenuity. He was not talking about armies. He was talking about this: the daily, ordinary, unglamorous choice not to add pain to a world that already has enough.

Be gentle. With others. And fiercely, relentlessly, unapologetically — with yourself.

Ahiṃsā अहिंसा

Ahiṃsā is the first and highest ethical principle in Jainism, Hinduism, and Buddhism. It means non-harm, non-violence — not just the absence of physical violence, but the absence of harm in thought, word, and deed.

Modern psychology confirms what ancient traditions knew: harm ripples outward. A single act of cruelty can cascade through a community. But so can a single act of kindness. Both are contagious.

Ahimsa toward yourself is equally important. The way you speak to yourself in your own mind is the foundation of how you treat others.
