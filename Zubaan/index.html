Zubān

the tongue

Zubaan is the tongue — the organ of language and the organ of promise. When someone says “meri zubaan” they mean “my word,” and when it is broken, something more than a promise dies. The tongue builds entire worlds with its syllables and razes them with a single sentence. It is the smallest muscle that causes the largest wars. Zubān asks you to name the words that constructed or demolished your life — the sentences spoken to you or by you that altered everything that came after.

Zubān

You remember the exact words. Not the room, not the weather, not what anyone was wearing — but the words. The sentence a teacher said that made you believe you were intelligent. The thing a parent said in anger that you have been trying to disprove for thirty years. The confession that cracked open a friendship. The lie that sealed a fate. Words are not air. They are architecture.

Zubaan is also promise — the pledge made with nothing but breath and intention. In a culture where a person's word was their contract, a broken zubaan was not a legal matter but a moral collapse. To give your zubaan was to put your entire self behind a sentence. To break it was to break something inside the language itself.
