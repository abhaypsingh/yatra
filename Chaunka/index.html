A chaunka is the sacred kitchen space — the platform where food is prepared, where the floor is washed clean before cooking begins. In traditional homes, you remove your shoes before entering the chaunka. Food is not just cooked here — it is offered. Chaunkā asks you to name the fires around which your family nourished itself — the rituals of feeding that were also rituals of love.

Chaunkā

The chaunka is not a stove. It is the entire sacred space of food preparation. The floor is wiped with a wet cloth. Hands are washed. The first roti goes to the fire, to the cow, to the guest — never to oneself. This is the Indian kitchen’s deepest teaching: that cooking is service, not just survival.

Your chaunkas are the rituals of nourishment that fed more than bodies. The family dinners that fed belonging. The lunchbox packed with love that fed a child’s confidence. The meal shared with a stranger that fed your own humanity.
