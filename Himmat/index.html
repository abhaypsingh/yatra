the courage

Himmat is not the absence of fear — it is the decision to move while fear is still screaming. It is the hand that reaches for the door, the voice that speaks when the room is hostile, the body that gets up one more morning when getting up feels impossible. Himmat asks you to name the moments when you gathered courage — not because fear had passed, but because something mattered more than the fear.

Himmat

Courage is almost always invisible to the person performing it. In the moment, it does not feel heroic. It feels like nausea, like shaking hands, like a voice that wobbles. You do the difficult thing not because you feel brave but because the alternative — the regret of inaction — has become more frightening than the action itself.

The world celebrates grand courage: the soldier, the activist, the rescuer. But most himmat is domestic and undocumented. The first day at a new school in a new city. The conversation where you finally said what had been eating you alive. The morning you got out of bed after weeks of not wanting to. No one gave you a medal. No one even noticed. But you know what it cost.
