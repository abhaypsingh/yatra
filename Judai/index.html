Judāī

the separation

Judai is the space that opens when what you love is taken away — by distance, by death, by a decision that could not be undone. It is not the same as loss. Loss has finality. Judai has longing — because sometimes the separated thing still exists, somewhere, just not with you. Judāī asks you to name the separations that carved hollows in your life — the partings that were chosen and the ones that were forced upon you.

Judāī

Separation is felt first in the body. The hand reaches for someone who is not there. The ears strain for a voice that used to fill the room. The routines built for two collapse into rituals of one — cooking less food, sleeping on one side of the bed, forgetting, for a moment, and then remembering. The remembering is the worst part.

Some judai is imposed: a parent sent away for work, a child who leaves for a life elsewhere, a loved one claimed by illness or geography. Other separations are chosen — the necessary leaving that breaks your heart but preserves your survival. In both cases, what remains is the outline of presence, like the shadow on a wall after the picture has been removed.

Name the separations you have weathered. Each one is proof that you loved something enough for its absence to reshape you.
