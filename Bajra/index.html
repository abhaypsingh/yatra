Bajrā

the millet

Bajrā is pearl millet — the grain that grows where wheat and rice cannot. It survives drought, poor soil, and scorching heat. It asks for almost nothing and gives solid, sustaining nourishment in return. In Rajasthan, bajrā roti with ghee and red chilli is the food that built generations of endurance. It is not glamorous. It is not refined. It simply keeps you alive when nothing else will. Bajrā asks you to name the humble, unglamorous things that sustained you through your droughts — the plain nourishments that kept you going when finer things were unavailable.

Bajrā

Bajrā does not advertise itself. It does not appear on restaurant menus or in food magazines. It is the grain of the poor, the rural, the enduring. And yet — it is one of the oldest cultivated grains on earth, tougher and more resilient than any of the grains that replaced it in modern kitchens. When the soil is too dry for rice and the heat too fierce for wheat, bajrā grows anyway. Quietly. Without complaint.

Your bajrā was the sustenance that no one celebrated but that kept you alive. The routine that held your days together. The small act of discipline that prevented collapse. The person who wasn’t exciting but was unfailingly present. Not the feast — the daily bread. Not the cure — the thing that kept you going until a cure arrived.
