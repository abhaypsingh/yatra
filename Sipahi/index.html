A sipahi is not a general. He does not make the plan or give the order. He stands at the post he is assigned, holds the line he is given, and does not leave until relieved. The sipahi’s virtue is not glory — it is reliability. History remembers generals. It does not remember the sipahi who stood at the gate through the night, who marched in the dust without knowing the strategy, who held a position simply because he was told to. Yet every victory belongs to the sipahi more than the general — because victory is built from a thousand small holdings, not one great command.

You have been a sipahi. The eldest child who held the family line. The employee who did the unglamorous work. The friend who showed up every time, not because it was exciting but because you were needed.
