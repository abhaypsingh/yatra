Ārsī

the mirror ring

An ārsī is a tiny mirror set into a ring, worn on the thumb by brides so they can glimpse their own face without turning to a looking glass. It is the smallest mirror in the world — just large enough to see one eye, one curve of cheek. You must look deliberately, at just the right angle, to catch your reflection. Ārsī asks you to name the tiny, unexpected mirrors in which you caught a glimpse of your true self — a remark, a photograph, a child’s imitation of you, a stranger’s reaction.

Ārsī

The ārsī is proof that you do not need a large mirror to see yourself. A fragment is enough — sometimes more honest than the full-length version. The bride wearing the ārsī sees herself in glimpses: a flash of kohl-lined eye, a curve of smile. She pieces herself together from fragments, and the portrait is truer for being incomplete.

Your mirror rings were the accidental reflections. The friend who said “you always do that” and suddenly you saw a habit you never noticed. The recording of your own voice that sounded nothing like what you hear inside your head. The child who played “house” and you recognised your own tone in their pretend scolding.

Name the mirrors. The truest self-portraits are painted by accident, in mirrors too small to lie.
