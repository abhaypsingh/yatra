Insaaf is justice — but justice sought and justice received are rarely the same thing. The verdict you wanted and the verdict you got often wear different faces. Sometimes justice arrives late, disguised as consequence; sometimes it never arrives at all. Insāf asks you to name the justices you sought and the forms in which they arrived — or the silence where they should have been.

Insāf

The cry of “insaaf” echoes through Indian courts, streets, and homes alike. It is one of the most ancient human demands — that the wrong be made right, that the scales be balanced, that someone, somewhere, acknowledge what happened. But insaaf in life is rarely the clean verdict of a courtroom. It is messy, partial, delayed, or entirely absent.

You have sought insaaf. For a childhood unfairness. For a betrayal in love. For a professional injustice. Some of those cases were settled by time, some by forgiveness, some by the quiet realisation that justice and peace are not always the same thing. And some remain open, the verdict still pending.

Name the verdicts — delivered and undelivered. The seeking itself is a form of honour.
