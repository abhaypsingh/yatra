Ghū̃ghaṭ

the drawn veil

Ghū̃ghaṭ is the veil drawn over the face — not a wall but a curtain, not a refusal but a deferral. Behind the ghū̃ghaṭ, the face still exists; it is simply not shown. The veil says: I am here, but not all of me is for your eyes right now. It is a boundary made of cloth, a privacy made of gesture. Ghū̃ghaṭ asks you to name the veils you have drawn — the parts of yourself you chose to conceal, not from shame but from a sense that some things are too tender to show to every gaze.

What did you veil — not from shame, but from tenderness?

Ghū̃ghaṭ

The ghū̃ghaṭ is often misread as suppression, but it can also be sovereignty. To choose what to reveal and what to withhold is an act of self-possession. The bride behind the ghū̃ghaṭ is not absent; she is present on her own terms. The veil says: you will see me when the moment is right, and that timing is mine to decide.

Your veils were the chosen concealments. The emotions you carried without displaying. The knowledge you held without announcing. The wounds you dressed in private. These were not lies — they were boundaries. Not every truth needs to be visible to every person at every moment.

Name the veils. They protected what was most tender in you until the right eyes were ready to see.
