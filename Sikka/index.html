the coin

A sikka has two faces — value depends on which side you see. It is the smallest unit of exchange, yet it carries the full weight of a transaction. Every relationship, every choice, every moment has two sides, and you never see both at once. Sikkā asks you to name the currencies exchanged between people — the trades of time, attention, sacrifice, and loyalty that define your bonds.

Sikkā

In bazaars across India, the sikka passes from hand to hand — warm from one palm, cool in the next. The coin itself is neutral, but what it buys is never neutral. A coin given to a beggar is charity. The same coin given to a child is a game. The same coin placed in a temple is prayer. The sikka is the medium; the meaning comes from the exchange.

Between people, the currencies are more complex. You pay in attention and receive in loyalty. You spend years and earn trust. You invest patience and sometimes get nothing in return. Not every exchange is fair, but every exchange teaches you the true price of things.
