Sandlī

the low stool

A sandlī is the small, low wooden stool found in every Indian kitchen and courtyard. It stands barely a hand’s height off the ground. You sit on it to chop vegetables, to wash clothes, to feed a child, to tend the fire. It brings you close to the earth, close to the work, close to the ground where real life happens. Sandlī asks you to name the low seats you have taken — the positions of humility, closeness, and grounded presence where you did the work that mattered most.

Sandlī

The sandlī is the opposite of a throne. A throne elevates you above others. A sandlī brings you down to the level of the work, the child, the fire. In every Indian household, the most important work — cooking, cleaning, feeding, mending — is done from this low seat. The person on the sandlī is not diminished by the low position; they are made essential. The meal cannot be cooked from a chair. The child cannot be bathed from a standing height.

Your sandlīs were the humble positions you chose. The times you got low, got close, got your hands in the work. The conversations where you listened from below rather than spoke from above. The tasks that required you to kneel, to bend, to come down to earth.
