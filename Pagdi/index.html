Pagdī

the turban

A pagdī is a turban — the cloth wound around the head that signifies honour, responsibility, and social standing. To place a pagdī on someone’s head is to invest them with authority. To remove it publicly is the deepest insult. The pagdī is not merely worn; it is earned, inherited, or bestowed. It says: this person carries the weight of a family, a community, a promise. Pagdī asks you to name the honours that were placed on your head — the responsibilities you carry, the roles you were given, and the weight of being the one others count on.

Pagdī

In Rajasthani and Marwari culture, the pagdī ceremony is one of the most solemn rituals. When a patriarch dies, the eldest son sits before the community and has the turban placed on his head. In that moment, every debt, every promise, every obligation of the previous generation transfers to him. The turban is not a crown — it is a yoke disguised as an honour.

Your pagdīs were the roles placed upon you. Becoming a parent. Becoming the breadwinner. Becoming the one everyone calls in a crisis. Becoming the keeper of a family’s story. These were not always chosen; sometimes the turban was simply placed on your head, and you were expected to wear it. The honour and the burden arrived as one.

Name the turbans. They sit on your head whether or not anyone sees them, and their weight is real.
