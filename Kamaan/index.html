A kamaan works through tension. The more you pull it back, the farther the arrow flies. Without resistance, there is no release. Without the backward pull, there is no forward launch. Kamān asks you to name the tensions you have drawn in your life — the moments of pulling back that preceded your farthest launches, the resistances that were preparation, not failure.

Kamān

The bow teaches: going backward is part of going forward. The arrow must be drawn back before it can fly. The string must be pulled away from the target before it can propel towards it. In the moment of maximum tension, everything feels wrong — you are pointed at the ground, pulled away from where you want to go. But that is the moment of greatest potential.

Your kamaan moments were the times life pulled you back. The failures that stored energy. The waiting that built capacity. The tensions that, when released, launched you farther than you could have gone from a standing start.

Name the bows. They are the tensions that became trajectories.
