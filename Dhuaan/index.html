Smoke is the signal before the fire — where there is smoke, something is burning. It rises before the flame is visible, a warning written in the air for anyone willing to look up. Dhuā̃ asks you to name the warning signals you saw rising before the crisis — the smoke you noticed, the smoke you ignored, and the smoke you wish someone else had seen.

Dhuā̃

The village saying is simple: where there is smoke, there is fire. But in life the smoke often rises slowly — a change in someone’s tone, a pattern of small withdrawals, a discomfort you cannot name. You see it and you tell yourself it is nothing. The smoke thickens. And then the fire arrives, and you realise the warning was there all along.

Looking back, the smoke was always visible. The signs before a relationship ended. The tension before a family fractured. The weariness before you broke. Not all smoke leads to catastrophe — sometimes you caught it in time. But every fire had its smoke.
