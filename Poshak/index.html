the garment

A poshak is not just clothing — it is identity worn on the body. The uniform that makes you a soldier. The white kurta that makes you a mourner. The wedding red that makes you a bride. Clothes do not just cover — they declare. Poshāk asks you to name the identities you have worn — the roles that fit like garments, some chosen, some inherited, some outgrown.

Poshāk

In India, clothing is rarely just clothing. A white saree signals widowhood. Saffron signals renunciation. The school uniform says you belong somewhere. The poshak is the visible declaration of an invisible identity — and sometimes, the garment shapes the wearer more than the wearer shapes the garment.

You have worn many poshaks. The dutiful child. The rebellious teenager. The professional. The parent. Some fit perfectly. Some were tailored by someone else. Some you wore so long you forgot they were costumes.

Name the garments. Know which ones you chose and which ones chose you.
