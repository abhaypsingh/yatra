Kafan

The kafan teaches the one lesson no living person wants to learn: that the body you loved — the hands that fed you, the face that smiled at you across rooms, the chest where you rested your head — was always temporary. The cloth is simple and unadorned because decoration has lost its meaning. What remains is the shape beneath it, and even that will change.

You have stood near this finality. Perhaps you washed the body or watched others do it. Perhaps you placed flowers or earth. Perhaps you simply stood and felt the weight of a silence that no words could fill. These encounters with death are not morbid — they are the most honest moments of a life, the instants when pretence becomes impossible and grief strips you down to your truest self.

Name the endings you have witnessed. Not to dwell in sorrow, but to honour the dead by remembering that you were there — that they were not alone when the shroud was draped.
