तामझाम

Tāmjhām

the grand display

Tāmjhām means pomp and show — the elaborate arrangements made to impress, the grand spectacle put on for an audience. A wedding with a thousand guests. A welcome with flower garlands and a brass band. The word carries both admiration and gentle irony: all this tāmjhām, and for what? Tāmjhām asks you to name the grand displays you have witnessed or created — and to look honestly at what lay behind the spectacle. Was there substance beneath the show, or was the show itself the substance?

Tāmjhām

Tāmjhām is neither purely positive nor purely negative. Sometimes the grand display is genuine celebration — the community coming together to honour a moment that deserves all the flowers and music. Sometimes it is armour — the louder the spectacle, the deeper the insecurity it conceals. And sometimes it is love expressed in the only language available: my grandmother could not say “I love you,” but she could cook for forty people and lay out silver plates.

Your tāmjhāms were the grand productions of your life. The events you over-planned. The gestures that were larger than necessary. The displays others put on that dazzled or overwhelmed you. In each case, the question is the same: what was the show really about?

Name the spectacles. Behind every grand display is a human being who wanted something badly enough to build a stage for it.
