Zeher

In the old stories, poison was always dramatic — a vial in a ring, a coated blade, a cup offered by a smiling enemy. But the real poisons of a life are quieter. They are the comparison that eats your contentment. The resentment you swallow every day at the same table. The belief planted in childhood that you are fundamentally flawed. These poisons do not kill quickly. They work over years, over decades, dissolving things you did not know you needed until they were gone.

Some poisons you chose. The substance, the relationship, the pattern you returned to knowing it would hurt. Others were administered without your consent — the cruelty of a parent, the culture that told you to shrink, the system that ground you down. Naming the poison is the first step toward finding the antidote.

Name what poisoned you. Not to dwell in it, but to finally see it clearly.
