Chirāgh

the oil lamp

A chiragh burns by consuming itself. The oil is drawn up the wick, and the wick burns slowly, turning fuel into light. It illuminates the room by diminishing itself. Yet it is never pitied — it is honoured, lit at thresholds and altars. Chirāgh asks you to name the lights you have lit in your life by consuming your own resources — the generosities that cost you something and illuminated others.

What did you illuminate by consuming yourself?

Chirāgh

The chiragh does not resent its burning. It was made for this. Oil without a wick is just oil in a dish. A wick without flame is just thread. But together, burning, they become light. The chiragh teaches that purpose and cost are the same thing. To give light, something must be consumed.

You have been a chiragh. The sleepless nights that gave your child security. The effort that gave your team success. The patience that gave your partner peace. You burned — and the room was brighter because you did.

Name the lamps. They are the gifts that cost you something real.
