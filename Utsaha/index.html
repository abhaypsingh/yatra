Utsāha

hope

Hope is not optimism. Optimism says everything will be fine. Hope says: I do not know if it will be fine, but I will keep going anyway.

That is braver.

Six dark rooms. In each one, a thread of light. Your job is not to make the room bright. Just find the thread.

You found the light.

Not because the rooms were not dark. They were. Not because you pretended they were bright. You did not. But because you looked anyway.

Hope is not the belief that suffering will end. It is the decision that suffering is not all there is. That somewhere, in every dark room, there is a crack where the light gets in. And that you are the kind of person who looks for it.

That is not naive. That is the bravest thing a human being can do.

Utsāha उत्साह

Utsāha means enthusiasm, vigor, hope, perseverance. In the Bhagavad Gītā, it is listed as one of the divine qualities — the energy that keeps you moving when the path is unclear.

Psychologist Charles Snyder defined hope as having three parts: a goal (something you want), pathways (ways to get there), and agency (the belief that you can take those paths). Hope is not passive wishing. It is active problem-solving combined with the refusal to give up.

You do not need to see the whole staircase. Just the next step.

Everything stays on your device. Nothing is sent anywhere.
