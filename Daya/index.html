self-compassion

You would never talk to a friend the way you talk to yourself.

The voice inside that says you are not enough — it learned those words somewhere. They are not the truth.

Six moments where the inner critic is loudest. Learn to answer it — not with silence, but with kindness.

but if a friend told you this, you would say:

You were kind to yourself.

The voice that criticizes you is loud. But it is not the only voice you have. Somewhere inside, there is a quieter voice — the one that speaks to your friends with gentleness, that forgives others easily, that knows mistakes are not the end of the world.

That voice belongs to you too. It always has. You just forgot to aim it inward.

The next time the critic speaks, pause. Ask yourself: would I say this to someone I love? If the answer is no, then you do not deserve to hear it either.

Dayā दया

Dayā means compassion, mercy, tenderness. In Indian tradition, dayā is considered the highest form of dharma — and it begins with how you treat yourself.

Dr. Kristin Neff’s research on self-compassion shows three components: self-kindness instead of self-judgment, common humanity instead of isolation, and mindfulness instead of over-identification with thoughts. People who practice self-compassion are not weaker — they are more resilient.

You deserve the same kindness you give to others.
