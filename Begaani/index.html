the estrangement

Begaani is the slow discovery that someone who once knew you completely has become a stranger — or that you have become a stranger to a place, a family, a version of yourself. The distance was not sudden. It grew one unanswered call at a time. Begānī asks you to name the estrangements in your life — the relationships and connections that faded until familiarity became foreignness.

Begānī

Estrangement does not arrive with a declaration. No one says: from this day forward, we are strangers. Instead, it creeps. A visit is postponed. A phone call is not returned. The conversation that once flowed without effort now requires planning, and the planning itself feels like evidence that something essential has been lost.

You return to your childhood home and the rooms feel smaller, the streets feel wrong. You sit across from someone you loved for years and realise you no longer know what they think about anything that matters. The estrangement is not anger — anger at least implies engagement. It is the quiet withdrawal of recognition, the moment when someone you knew by heart becomes someone you know only by name.

Name the estrangements you have lived through. They are not failures — they are the natural cost of a life in motion, where growth sometimes means growing apart.
