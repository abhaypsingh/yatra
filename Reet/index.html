A reet is a custom — the way things are done because they have always been done that way. Some reets carry wisdom compressed by generations. Others carry prejudice preserved by habit. Rīt asks you to name the customs you followed and the ones you chose to break.

Rīt

Tradition is both treasure and trap. The reet that says “we gather every Sunday for a meal” carries the wisdom of belonging. The reet that says “daughters do not inherit” carries the poison of exclusion. And the difficulty is that both arrive in the same package — the way things have always been done — and you must discern which to keep and which to discard.

Breaking a reet takes courage, because custom has the weight of every ancestor behind it. But keeping a reet also takes courage, because the modern world will call you old-fashioned for honouring what your grandparents honoured.

Name the customs. Whether followed or broken, they shaped who you became.
