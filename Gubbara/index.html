the balloon

A gubbara is a child’s first experience of joy and loss happening in the same breath. You hold the string, you watch it pull upward, and then — it slips or it bursts. Either way, the lightness was real while it lasted. Gubbarā asks you to name the joys that were beautiful precisely because they were fragile — the things that floated and then were gone.

Gubbarā

Every child at a mela holds a balloon like it is the most important thing in the world. And it is — for those minutes. The colour, the pull of the string, the way it bounces against the sky. Then the hand opens, or the balloon meets a thorn, and it is over. The child cries. But the joy was real.

You have had gubbara moments your whole life. Phases that felt like flying. People who made everything lighter. Plans that were perfect until they weren’t. The fragility was not a flaw — it was the condition of the beauty.

Name the balloons. Honour the floating, not just the falling.
