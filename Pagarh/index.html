Pagaṛh

the turban

A pagarh is not just cloth wound around the head — it is honour made visible. In Rajasthan, a man’s turban is his self-respect. To remove it is to surrender. To tie it on another is to crown them. The way it is wound, its colour, its height — each tells a story. Pagaṛh asks you to name the dignities you wear — the standards you visibly maintain that tell the world who you are and what you will not compromise.

Pagaṛh

In Rajasthan, they say: “Pagdi rakh, maan rakh” — keep the turban, keep the honour. The turban is not vanity. It is a public commitment to a standard. When you tie a turban, you are saying: I will behave in a way worthy of this cloth. It is a promise you make to yourself, worn where the world can see it.

You wear turbans. The professional standard you refuse to lower. The moral boundary you maintain visibly. The self-respect that is wound around you every morning, layer by layer, before you step outside.

Name the turbans. They are the dignities you choose to wear in public.
