Libās

the garment

A libaas is more than fabric. It is the self you show to the world — the costume you chose, the uniform forced upon you, the outfit that made you feel invincible or invisible. Libās asks you to recall the garments that mattered — clothes that disguised who you were, revealed who you wished to become, or carried the memory of an occasion so deeply that the fabric still holds the feeling.

Libās

We pretend clothes are trivial — surface, decoration, vanity. But the garment is the first language others read. A school uniform teaches obedience. A borrowed dress teaches humility. The outfit chosen for a first interview carries the entire weight of how you wish to be perceived by strangers who hold your future in their hands.

Some garments imprison. The clothing prescribed by convention — what a woman must wear, what a man must not. Others liberate. The first time you wore something that felt like the real you, the fabric became permission. And some garments outlive their wearers — a father’s coat hanging in a closet, still smelling of him, still holding the shape of his shoulders.

Remember the garments that mattered. Each one is a chapter of your body’s autobiography.
