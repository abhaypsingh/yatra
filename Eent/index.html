Āṇṭ

the brick

An eent is a single brick — clay shaped by hand, fired in a kiln, then laid alongside thousands of others to become a wall, a house, a temple. No one admires a single brick. But without each single brick, there is no structure. The brick is the unit of patience: one at a time, one on top of another, until something stands. Āṇṭ asks you to name the single bricks of your life — the individual acts, days, and decisions that, laid one atop another, built the structures you now inhabit.

Āṇṭ

The brick asks nothing of the architect. It does not need to understand the blueprint. It only needs to be placed well, with mortar, in its position. The mason lays one brick, then another, then another. The wall reveals itself only after hundreds of bricks. The structure becomes visible only after thousands. This is how most lives are built — not by grand gestures but by daily placements.

Your bricks were the repeated small acts. Getting up early every day. Saying “I love you” even when you were tired. Showing up to work. Practising when nobody was watching. Each brick was unremarkable. The structure they built was extraordinary.

Name the bricks. The most impressive structures in your life were built one unremarkable day at a time.
